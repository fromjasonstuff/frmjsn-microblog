Nov 24, 2023

I’m using defined tags to filter my public bookmarks. Posts with lots of ads will be tagged as “adicles.”

The “posts” tag is exclusive to personal “sites.” “Articles” come from “orgs” and “agencies,” so forth, and so on.

Occasionally, I’d like to share a piece from a big publication, like: As We May Think (1945) by Vannevar Bush for The Atlantic.

Because of all the ads, that page is not a “post” or an “article” in fromjason land. It’s a piece of revenue-generating content with a degraded reading experience (of no fault of the author, obviously).

So, I’m adding an “adicle” tag for such occasions.

Adicles exist solely to generate ad revenue with no considerations for the reading experience (or substance in many cases). Still, some adicles contain writing worth a share. I’ll just be sure to label it what it is: ads with a splash of prose.

Writing is thinking. iA Writer is designed to make thinking enjoyable. A writing app that thinks for you is a robot that does your jogging.

What surprised me the most about our new AI-capable world is the expressed disdain for human creators (artists) by “AI artists” (prompters). There’s this attitude that creativity itself has been an injustice to non-creative people. And that AI is here to square that circle.

People who sell art for profit, but cannot experience the joys of creativity, view artistry as a liability. To them, it’s nothing more than a supply chain bottleneck. It’s manual labor to be automated away.

But of course, the economic efficiencies promised by generative AI isn’t enough for them. “Prompt artists” demand all the creative accolades too. They want the gold medal placed around their necks after their robot finishes the race.
